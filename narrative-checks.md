# Narrative ("Global View") Checks

Use these when editing anything longer than a standalone sentence: a paragraph, a subsection, a section, or a full paper. **Scope the checks to the unit under review** — if the user gives one paragraph, "the whole" means that paragraph; if they give a section, it means that section; if they give the full paper, it means the paper. Where a check requires the broader document (e.g. comparing a paragraph against the abstract), read the surrounding material before judging.

**You must run all four groups below, and every sub-point within each group, in order.** Name each group and each sub-point explicitly, state how it applies to the unit under review, and report whether it surfaces an issue. Do not skip, merge, or abbreviate any point; if one does not apply, write "not applicable" and why.

## Group 1 — Core message and framing

- **Central message** — the unit builds toward one clear takeaway, stateable in a single sentence. Identify it; flag the unit if no single message emerges or if multiple competing messages dilute it.
- **Framing alignment** — the unit's framing is consistent with how the work is set up elsewhere (for a full paper: title, abstract, introduction, and conclusion all promise and deliver the same thing). Flag mismatches between what is promised and what is delivered.
- **Result framing and novelty** — main results are clearly highlighted and distinguished from supporting or secondary material, and their novelty relative to prior work is clear. Flag buried results or unclear contributions.

## Group 2 — Logical arc and motivation

- **Logical arc** — each part (sentence-cluster, paragraph, or section, as appropriate to scope) is motivated by and follows from what precedes it; the ordering is purposeful. Flag gaps, non-sequiturs, or steps that arrive before their motivation.
- **Motivation and stakes** — the problem and the gap it addresses are set up before the technical content, and that thread is sustained throughout the unit. Flag technical material whose purpose the reader cannot locate.
- **Signposting and transitions** — openers, summaries, roadmap statements, and forward/back references guide the reader between parts. Flag abrupt transitions and missing connective tissue.

## Group 3 — Consistency and economy

- **Cross-part consistency** — notation, terminology, and claims stay consistent across the unit (and with the rest of the paper); every promise made earlier is paid off later. Flag drifting notation, renamed concepts, and unmet promises.
- **Redundancy and balance** — no content is repeated across parts without purpose; length is proportioned to importance; material sits in the right place (main text vs. appendix, or the right section). Flag duplication, bloat, and misplaced material.

## Group 4 — Claims and audience

- **Scope of claims** — conclusions match what was actually shown, with no over- or under-claiming; relevant limitations are acknowledged. Flag claims that outrun the evidence and results that are undersold.
- **Audience and entry points** — the unit is accessible to the paper's target reader; required background is introduced before it is relied upon. Flag prerequisites used before they are established and entry points that assume too much.
